This project addresses a critical socioeconomic question: What factors drive women's employment rates across different countries and regions? Using advanced machine learning techniques and a comprehensive dataset of economic, educational, and social indicators, I've created predictive models that identify the most significant barriers to women's workforce participation and suggest evidence-based policy interventions that could lead to greater gender equality in employment.

Project Overview

The Women Employment Prediction model utilizes machine learning to identify the most influential factors determining female labor force participation across 193 countries. By analyzing social, economic, educational, and policy variables, the model provides insights to guide targeted interventions that can effectively increase women's employment rates.

Key Research Questions

  • Which socioeconomic factors most strongly predict women's employment rates?
  • How do these factors vary across countries with different development levels?
  • What policy interventions might be most effective in increasing women's workforce participation?
  • Can we build a model that accurately predicts employment rates based on country indicators?

Most Influential Factors

The model identified the following factors as having the strongest influence on women's employment rates:

  1. Childcare Accessibility: Countries with affordable, widely available childcare show significantly higher women's employment.
  2. Tertiary Education: Female education at university level has a strong positive correlation with workforce participation.
  3. Maternity/Parental Leave Policies: Paid parental leave shows a strong positive impact on women returning to work.
  4. Gender Pay Gap: Smaller pay differences correlate with higher female employment rates.
  5. Cultural Attitudes: Societal norms regarding gender roles significantly impact participation rates.

Regional Insights

High-Income Countries

In developed economies, the availability of affordable childcare and flexible work arrangements emerged as the strongest predictors of women's employment rates. Countries with comprehensive family-friendly policies consistently show higher participation rates.

Middle-Income Countries

For emerging economies, educational attainment—particularly tertiary education—and urbanization were the most significant factors. The transition from agricultural to service-based economies appears to create new opportunities for female employment.

Low-Income Countries

In developing regions, basic education access, fertility rates, and agricultural productivity emerged as key predictors. Interestingly, necessity often drives higher participation in informal employment despite cultural barriers.
